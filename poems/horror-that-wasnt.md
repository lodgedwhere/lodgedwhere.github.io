**The Horror That Wasn’t**

``` 
When he stepped from the trees
with the shining blade
and the dull bludgeon,
and there was no escape —
trapped,
check-mated —

everything in me stopped
except the truth.

A flash —
not a life review,
but a single, perfect thread:
the turn down the road,
the drive toward solitude,
the quiet inevitability
of arriving exactly here.

Then the world sharpened
to a point.

I saw the grass
already appointed to bear
blood, meat, and bone —
my blood,
my meat,
my bones —
and the birds’ bright songs
waiting to braid themselves
with whatever cry
my throat might offer.

Yet there was
no terror.
Not even dread.
Only seeing.
Only presence.
Only the truth that stands
when the story falls silent.

In that moment
something I had always believed
quietly died:
the certainty that death
must be horror,
that its shadow
was absolute.

But when I stood
at its threshold,
I found no monster —
only reality,
utterly simple,
utterly clear.

I realized how little
Nature had ever noticed me:
my bowels digesting every meal,
my heart pumping its million gallons,
faithfully,
without interest in the tale
I told myself
about fear,
about endings,
about what must never be faced.

And then,
at the very brink
of what I thought
would be the ultimate darkness,
I found light instead —
a quiet, wordless truth:

What we flee from most
is often what frees us.
What we dread
is sometimes the doorway
into the life that follows.
And the moment of death
may not be a horror,
but an unveiling.

The clearing taught me
what nothing else could:
that the thing we fear
above all else
can open us to a new understanding—
and did.

```
