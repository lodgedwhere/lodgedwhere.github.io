**Meaning turns sensation into suffering**

A raw sensation is not yet “pain” or "suffering" in the full human sense. First there is just an appearance, a signal. Then almost instantly, memory recognizes its pattern, places it within the mapped image of the body, or the constructed social self with its desires and fears, and relates it to a meaning: this is happening to me, here. That second movement is what turns sensation into suffering as an experienced fact.

So pain and suffering is not merely intensity. It is intensity plus interpretation, plus location, plus ownership. The body-memory system says: this shape of signal belongs to this region, this region belongs to my body, and this condition matters. In that sense, pain is already a constructed relation.

This also suggests that what feels immediate is actually layered. There is the bare event, and then there is the organization of that event into bodily meaning. Memory supplies the context, and context supplies the suffering. Without that relational framing, there may still be sensation, even violent sensation, but not yet the same thing we call pain.

Thus meaning is not inherent in the signal, but rather arises when distinction, memory, and identification are brought to bear on it. Pain is not only felt -- it is *recognized*. And recognition already contains a world.
