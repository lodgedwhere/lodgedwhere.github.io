**Throw away the key**

The sense of a personal past, along with the ego that evaluates it, arises within the same karmic fabric. Memory, judgment, regret, justification — each is constructed inside an imagined world of discrete “things.” When ego compares itself to the past, it is karma interpreting karma, a closed loop with no external reference point. In such a system, correspondence and difference carry no intrinsic meaning. Meaning itself is part of the fiction.

Seeing this dissolves the idea that karma teaches, redeems, or condemns. Nothing is learned, resolved, or corrected, because the ledger was never real. Even the impulse to reconcile — to make sense of what was — is simply another karmic motion. Agreement with the past and rejection of it are equally empty gestures, each presuming a tribunal that does not exist.

From here, it is clear that the ego has no essential purpose. It was clung to as a means of deciphering an otherwise unintelligible experience into relative meaning. But once meaning is seen to be empty, ego is like a precious key to a door that was never locked — unnecessary, and burdensome to perpetually reify.

The final release is the abandonment of orientation altogether — the stance of one who could miss something, who knows what is “happening”. Nothing needs to be watched, guarded, or purified. What remains is not effort or insight, but naked immediacy.

This clarity also dissolves the fear of death. The mistake was never existence, only reification. “I” was imagined as a thing among things, destined to become no-thing. But the true “I” was never an object that could be lost or preserved. It is the source from which meaning itself arises — the condition that allows “thing,” “no-thing,” life, and death to appear at all. Meaning does not point to it; meaning emerges from it.

Thus death cannot touch it, not because it is immortal, but because it was never alive in the way objects are alive. Life cannot confirm it, and death cannot negate it. When this is seen, the search ends quietly. Not in annihilation, not in transcendence, but in the simple recognition of what was never absent.
