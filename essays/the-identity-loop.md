**The identity loop**

What is ordinarily taken to be the self is not found as an object in experience. It is not seen, heard, or touched. It does not appear in the field alongside other phenomena. And yet it seems undeniably present, persistent, continuous, and central.

This apparent presence rests on a subtle mechanism: a recurring loop of thought.

At its simplest, the loop takes the form of a problem being posed, a search beginning, a resolution being reached, and a temporary subsiding of tension before the cycle restarts. The specific content varies endlessly. It may appear as “Am I ok?”, “What should I do?”, “What does this mean?”, or “How do I resolve this?” Despite these differences, the structure remains the same. Each iteration implies a problem for someone, a search by someone, and a resolution achieved by someone.

That “someone” is never directly encountered. It is not given in experience. It is only inferred from the continuity of the loop.

This is the first layer of the illusion. The identity loop is taken as evidence of an “I” that runs it, that is described by it. But the direction of causality is reversed. There is no “I” behind the loop. The loop itself, the recursive referencing within thought, produces the appearance of one. When the loop is active, the “I” seems obvious. When the loop falls silent, no such entity is found.

Yet the mechanism runs deeper. Even in the absence of explicit problems, the ordinary functioning of thought continues. Experience is described, possibilities are imagined, language is formed, and meaning is organized. This activity appears neutral, but it carries a hidden structure.

All symbolic thought requires a point of reference. There must be a “here” from which “there” is distinguished, a “now” from which “then” is understood, and a “this” against which “that” is contrasted. This implicit reference point is not a word, but a structural necessity. It is an unspoken origin, a zero-point around which meaning is organized. It may be called the ur-symbol, “self”/“I”.

In every act of meaning, this reference point is silently invoked. Even without problem-solving, symbolic thought continuously reinforces the implied center by generating meaning. The “I” is therefore not only sustained by anxiety or searching, but by the ordinary act of making sense of experience.

And yet this center is never found. There is no experience of the ur-symbol itself, no direct encounter with the supposed origin. There are only thoughts referring to thoughts, symbols organizing symbols, and meaning arising through relational structure. All of this presupposes a center, but none of it reveals one.

This leads to a decisive inversion. It is not that there is an “I” which generates thought and meaning, but that thought and meaning generate the appearance of an “I.”

What remains outside this structure is not a new state, not a better version of the self, and not a final answer to be held. It is simply experience as it is, prior to being organized around a center.

In the absence of the loop, no problem is posed, no resolution is required, and no reference point is maintained. Yet nothing is missing. What remains is not blankness but immediacy. Sound appears without a listener. Sensation appears without an owner. Thought may arise, but it does not form a center. The Seer is the Seen… as Nisargadatta asserted, “The painter is in the painting”.

Happiness, in this light, is not the successful completion of the loop. It is not, “Am I ok? … I am ok.” It is what is revealed when the need to ask that question does not arise.

The identity loop may continue to operate. Language continues, thought continues, meaning continues. But the crucial shift is that none of it is taken as evidence that there is someone at the center of it.

The ur-symbol “I” remains functionally necessary for symbolic thought, but it is seen to be existentially empty. In that recognition, the structure of the self is revealed not as an entity, but as a process that need not define what is.
