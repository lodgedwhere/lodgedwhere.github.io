**Boredom and the fiction of becoming**

Boredom is not a property of experience. It is a misattribution of authorship.

At its root lies a spontaneously arising question: “what next?”. It appears impersonally, like any other thought or sensation. On its own, this question carries no problem. It is simply an echo of habit, a remnant of orientation in time. The difficulty begins only when the question is claimed as mine: when it is assumed to arise from a “me” who must answer it.

This claim of ownership manufactures a role. A fictional “I” is posited as the source of the question, and immediately a task appears alongside it: the task of becoming something else. The present moment is reinterpreted as a staging area for a future improvement. From this single error confusing a spontaneous appearance for a personal demand, “boredom” is born.

Boredom is therefore not the absence of stimulation, meaning, or interest. It is the belief that the present moment is insufficient \*for someone\*. It is time smuggled into experience as dissatisfaction. The moment is not allowed to be complete; it is evaluated, judged, and found wanting by an imagined evaluator who stands apart from it.

But no such evaluator exists.

When the claim is withdrawn, when “what next?” is seen not as “my” question but as just another arising within experience, the structure collapses. There is no agent left who must act, no trajectory that must be advanced, no becoming that needs to be engineered. What remains is simply Being, already appearing as this exact configuration.

Nothing needs to be done. Nothing can be surrendered except the persistent belief in a separate “I” capable of standing outside the present and rendering verdicts upon it. Without that belief, boredom has nowhere to land. It cannot be found anywhere, ever.
