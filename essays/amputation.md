**The amputation**

I left my career as a physics professor when I realized that what I had called “understanding” was really only labeling, a projection of my own story onto the universe. Every equation, every theory saturated with my projected meaning, was another layer of conditional interpretation between me and what is. I had insisted that the apparently mute universe speak my language, when all along it *was* speaking to me — in silence. I simply wasn’t listening.

When the machinery of mind was finally seen as useless, I thought, almost involuntarily, “What hath God wrought?” Not as Morse meant it — a triumph of invention — but as a stunned recognition of the same divine hand revealed in ruin. For all my attempts to speak to the universe, it had never needed a telegraph. It was already here, resounding in silence.

My failure to believe in physics ultimately led to my greatest discovery: an unconditional love so deep that who I thought I was could die there. For years I had chased understanding, mistaking the brightness of intellect for light, desperately running from the void of unknowing toward what promised salvation just over the hill of another published paper. But when the equations failed to matter, when meaning itself slipped away, something else appeared: not an answer to a question, or a solution to a problem, but a presence. It asked for nothing, explained nothing. And in that vast openness, was absolute freedom. 

I write about this death to be rid of it. Despite what feels like progress in orienting life toward truth, some part of “me” still mourns, still persists in believing in the story. It’s the part that thinks it’s me, explaining to itself why this amputation was inevitable. Sometimes different versions of the story appear, each trying to reclaim the wound as its own. Suppressing them doesn’t work; they only whisper and scratch louder. So I listen to the universe and let them speak. I write these stories down, and watch them fade into the silence they were always meant to reveal.
