The oscillation of existential horror - and its transcendence

There is a moment, usually triggered by the thought of death, in which the mind turns inward and asks what, exactly, would be lost:
Who dies?
This question cannot be answered abstractly. It must be seen, and so forces attention into immediate experience — into the raw field of sensation prior to interpretation.
There, something unsettling is revealed:
Experience is undeniably present, yet no “self” can be found within it.
This condition of no-mind/no-self is not a philosophical conclusion but a direct contradiction. Something is happening, but not to anyone. The entire structure of meaning, which depends on a center, momentarily collapses.
This gives rise to a characteristic oscillation. On one side, there is the recognition of no-self, no-mind: a groundless immediacy without reference point. To the meaning-making system, this appears as horror — not because of what is present, but because nothing can be stabilized. Awareness flees in ignorance.
On the other side, the mind reflexively reconstructs the thought “I,” re-establishing a center to which experience belongs. But once seen, this reconstruction is transparent. It no longer convinces. It feels artificial, hollow — another kind of horror.
Thus the system oscillates:
between groundlessness and falseness,
between what cannot be held and what cannot be believed.
Within this oscillation, there is no stable resolution. The only apparent escape is self-deception — a re-immersion in narrative, distraction, and identification, allowing the illusion of self to regain its opacity. This is what is ordinarily called “living.”
But there is another possibility.
If, instead of fleeing, attention remains with the senses — without attempting to understand, interpret, generate meaning, or stabilize — the oscillation is not resolved by choosing a side, but by the failure of the entire structure that sustains it.
The “I” is not defeated; it simply ceases to be reconstituted.
What remains is not a new state, not an experience owned or maintained, but the ever-present condition in which all experiences arise: a groundless ground that cannot be thought, because thought itself depends on the distinctions that no longer organize it.
This is not something attained. It is what is revealed when nothing is added, and nothing is avoided.
