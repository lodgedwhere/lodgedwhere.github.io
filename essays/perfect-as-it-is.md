**Perfect as it is**

What appears as an unconscious civilization — devouring itself through end-stage capitalism, accelerating abstraction, and increasingly absurd politics — is not a tragic failure of history but a structurally complete movement whose deeper function is invisible to itself. As Thomas Aquinas wrote, “*if we consider the matter rightly, we shall see that all human occupations appear to serve those who contemplate the truth… Man’s ultimate happiness consists solely in the contemplation of God,*” a claim that, stripped of piety, means that labor, technology, governance, and even moral order exist to remove obstacles to stillness and recognition. Alan Watts diagnosed the modern crisis precisely as the collapse of symbolic meaning without the recognition of this deeper sufficiency, producing a hungry, need-driven Ouroboros of perpetual motion that consumes even the provisional meanings it creates. Yet this very exhaustion — this inability of substitutes to satisfy — is what clears the ground for contemplation to occur at all. Civilization does not awaken, does not liberate itself, and does not crown anyone as enlightened or superior; liberation is always personal, discontinuous, and undeserved. Seen this way, civilization is perfect not because it is wise or humane, but because, in consuming itself, it relentlessly removes what cannot ultimately satisfy, leaving (occasionally and quietly) the unobstructed possibility of seeing what was never absent.
