Symbolic Closure, and the Open Field of Reality

Symbolic systems close. This is not a limitation imposed from outside, but a requirement of their very function. A symbol operates by drawing a distinction and stabilizing it: this is this, not that. From such distinctions, relations can be built, references maintained, and loops completed. Even when symbolic systems become recursive, their recursion is governed. The rules of the system ensure that reentry resolves into identity, consistency, or at least local coherence. Closure is what allows symbols to mean anything at all.

But what appears—what is ordinarily called “reality”—does not share this constraint.

Here, distinctions are not fixed. Boundaries are not given once and for all. Reentry does not resolve into a final identity, but instead perpetuates the process that generated it. What emerges is not a closed loop, but an ongoing reappearance: patterns that sustain themselves without ever completing.

The contrast is subtle but decisive. A symbolic system must enforce closure in order to function; without it, reference collapses and meaning dissolves. But what appears does not depend on such enforcement. It does not require that distinctions stabilize into final forms. It does not require that loops terminate. It continues—not because it is complete, but because it does not close.

Within this open process, the sense of a stable center—the “I”—arises as a local attempt at closure. It maintains identity across time, anchors reference, and gives the appearance of a completed loop: a subject to whom experience belongs. But this stability is constructed. It is a recursive maintenance, not a fundamental feature. The “I” is a symbolic closure imposed on what does not, in itself, resolve.

When this mechanism is seen, the contrast becomes experiential. What once appeared as a coherent, continuous world centered on a self is revealed as something else: an open field of distinctions that do not settle into finality. Nothing concludes. Nothing stands as ultimate. What remains is not chaos, but an ongoing, self-sustaining appearance that does not require closure to persist.

Symbolic systems end. They resolve, terminate, or complete themselves according to their rules. But what appears does not end in this way. Its continuity is not the result of successful closure, but of its absence.
