**The cost of knowing**

The Whole cannot be known directly. To know is to distinguish, to take a part of what is undivided and use it as a ruler against the rest. Every act of knowledge depends on this prior cut. The ruler does not stand outside the Whole; it is made from it. Thus, all knowing is circular: the field is measured by a fragment that already presupposes a way of dividing it.

As knowledge grows, the ruler — a stable “I” — must grow with it. This reference is not given once and for all; it must be continuously maintained. The self is not a thing but an activity: the ongoing stabilization of the measuring frame.

But this maintenance is not without cost.

The senses already present a complete field. There is no inherent lack in perception itself. But mind does not operate separately from this field; mind projects onto it. Imagination, memory, and interpretation all reuse the same sensory capacity through which the world is given. They do not add a second channel; they occupy the existing one.

As a result, the construction and maintenance of the ruler partially occlude the field it seeks to measure. The more elaborate the structure of knowledge becomes, the more bandwidth is required to sustain it. The organism’s perceptual capacity is increasingly diverted into maintaining its own model of reality.

Thus, knowledge expands not by revealing more of the Whole, but by enlarging the structure that stands in relation to it.

This structure then claims to represent reality, while simultaneously limiting direct contact with it.

For basic functioning, only minimal distinctions are required: self and environment, movement and obstruction, signal and noise. But the recursive expansion of the ruler extends far beyond this. It seeks to stabilize identity, predict futures, construct narratives, and secure its own continuity. Each of these requires further projection, further occupation of the field.

The system becomes self-occupied.

The limit, then, is not imposed by the world, but by the activity of knowing itself. The more the system invests in maintaining and extending its model, the less of the unmediated field remains available.

What is commonly assumed is that perception is limited and thought extends it. But the inverse is closer to the truth:

Thought trades immediacy for structure.

And so, the paradox:

The more the system knows, the less it directly sees.
