**On the recursive generation of ego**

The maintained sense of an ego arises through a recursive structure in thought. When the mind imagines a scenario and poses the question “what would I do if…?”, it introduces a hypothetical situation and simultaneously posits an agent who will respond to it. The symbol “I” appears on both sides of the relation: the one who observes the situation and the one who acts within it. In this way, the agent is not discovered but conflated with a doer confined within time, produced by the very structure of the question.

In other words,

If I observe X → what would I do?

If I did Y → what would happen to me?

If I observe that happening → what should I do?

… etc.

Each cycle reinforces the presupposition that there must be someone inside the simulation making the decisions. But the “someone” *never appears outside the simulation itself.*

Because the same symbol occupies both roles, the distinction between observer and actor collapses into the identity “I = I.” This identity does not describe a substance; it marks the closure of a loop. Thought refers to a subject that is defined only by that act of reference. *The psychological self is merely a control variable in a predictive simulation.*

The ego is therefore not a fixed entity but the ongoing maintenance of this recursive relation. Each new act of anticipation, evaluation, or self-reference renews the loop, giving the appearance of a stable center of agency. In its simplest form, the structure can be expressed symbolically as a marked dynamic loop — *the Ouroboros* — a distinction that returns upon itself.

——

This insight into the recursive structure of ego did not appear first as a verbal idea. It began as a brief, nonverbal impression — a structural “seeing” that left almost no memory of its content, only a point-like trace that something had been perceived. The impression itself was not retained as an image or narrative.

Immediately afterward, the mind generated a dynamic analogy: a moving picture of a driven system forming a stable pattern, similar to how vortices emerge in a flowing fluid. This was followed by the appearance of language. Words formed that attempted to describe the dynamic analogy structure. Finally, those words were compared against the visual model, as though testing whether the symbolic description matched the original perception, but downstream of it.

The sequence therefore unfolded as: unremembered impression, dynamic analogy, language, and validation. The meaning expressed in language did not produce the insight; it arose later as a reconstruction of a structure that had already been perceived. The role of language was not to discover the insight but to compress and test it against the original, largely unremembered impression and represent it externally.

-----------

The mechanism of ego maintenance above still begs the question: where, when, and why does the loop start?

Being requires no knowledge of itself. But the mind seeks confirmation — the thought “I am.” This reflex is the atavistic reaction to the groundlessness of being, and constitutes the birth of mind itself, because the concept “I” makes the meaning of anything else possible by relation to it.

To know “I am,” the mind must construct a concept “I” and recognize it. This creates a recursive structure:

I = I

Yet recognizing that identity requires another act of knowing, expanding the loop:

I = (I = …)

Thus the self is not discovered but maintained by recursive re-entry — a conceptual loop built from memory and interpretation. Because this loop arises after experience, it never touches the concept-free ground of being itself.

The primordial impulse is simply to be. But when that impulse becomes the desire to know that one is, a conceptual self appears. That attempt to ground being in knowledge generates the recursive “I” — and with it, time, identity, and the machinery of suffering.

Being itself never required this verification.

--------------

The sense of ego may depend not only on the recursive structure of self-reference but also on the rate at which that loop is traversed. The mind repeatedly projects a hypothetical situation onto the sense field, allows it to dissolve, then projects an anticipated response and evaluates it from the standpoint of the same posited agent. When this cycle runs quickly, successive projections overlap and the gaps between them are not noticed, giving the impression of a continuous, stable “I.” When the loop slows, however, its phases become visible: the imagined scenario appears, dissolves, and is replaced by another, leaving intervals in which no center of reference is maintained. In this way the apparent solidity of ego may arise only when the recursive loop operates above a certain temporal density; below that rate the mechanism becomes perceptible and the sense of a persistent self feels insubstantial. Analogy to thresholds of structure formation in dissipative systems, such as the von Karman vortex street forming beyond a velocity threshold (referred to in the post), may be symbolically fruitful.
