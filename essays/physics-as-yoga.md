**Physics as yoga: failure redeemed**

Every precise system of knowledge begins on a foundational ground floor: everything must be made of something, so one begins by axiomatically positing the “stuff” of the theory. Mathematics does this with sets or categories, from which numbers and structures are built, explaining their supposed “origins.” Physics follows the same pattern, using formal constructs to model our experience of matter, and assigning meaning to equations that mirror the world’s behavior.

Yet reality is detailed at every scale we examine. Each supposed fundament reveals further structure beneath it. From bulk materials to atoms to nucleons to quarks and gluons to… There is no final layer, no ultimate indivisible “thing” — only turtles, all the way down.

Thus, for physics, a “theory of everything” is a contradiction in terms. Such a theory would require a complete description of an infinitely proliferating hierarchy of composites — an infinite regress of explanations, each built of something else. No matter how deep we dig, there is no bedrock “stuff” left to point to — only the recursive act of explanation itself.

But this endless regress is not a failure. It is the mirror held up to the mind that seeks finality. The futility of the search is the revelation: what collapses is not knowledge but the knower, who learns that every attempt to seize the Real as an object only confirms its absence. The “turtles all the way down” are not the world’s problem — they are the mind’s. The world does not ask to be explained. Only the mind demands closure.

Seen rightly, then, physics becomes a yoga — a discipline of discrimination so radical that it dissolves the very subject that discriminates. Each refinement of theory, each deeper probe into nature’s fabric, pares away another layer of the self’s projections. The physicist who persists to the end discovers not the final particle, but the vanishing point of the seeker himself.

And this is no consolation prize. The “grand prize” imagined by thought — the completed theory, the final equation, the God’s-eye view — would only perpetuate the illusion of separation between seer and seen. To transcend that illusion is to discover a wholeness beyond conception, where the need for explanation evaporates in direct presence.

The folly of the enterprise becomes its sanctity. For the same reason that all yogas begin in duality and end in unity, physics too can complete its arc only by consuming itself. Its true end is not the mastery of the world, but the surrender of the will to master. In that surrender, the universe reveals itself as already whole, already complete, already free of any “stuff” to be found.
