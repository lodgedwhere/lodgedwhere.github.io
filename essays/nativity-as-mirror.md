**Nativity as mirror**

The birth of Christ is not only a story about something that happened once.
It is a symbol of what is always happening — now.

Mary is called virgin not merely to describe a supposed miracle of biology,
but to name a condition:
a moment untouched by what came before,
unburdened by history,
free of justification.

Nothing is required for this birth.
No cause, no merit, no preparation.
The moment is immaculate because it is unconditional.

This is why the story echoes earlier ones:

Christ is Moses before the burning bush,
removing his shoes —
not to improve himself,
but to stop standing *on* the moment, claiming it,
and instead stand *within it*.
The ground is holy not because it is special,
but because nothing is standing between it and seeing itself.

Christ is Elijah in the wind, the earthquake, and the fire —
and then in the stillness that follows,
where striving ceases
and even silence is allowed to be enough.

In each case, nothing new is added.
Something *believed in* is simply dropped.

And so Christ’s birth is not an event that requires belief.
It is an invitation to recognition.

Before identity asserts itself,
before explanation rushes in,
before the need to deserve being appears —
there is simply presence, quietly aware of itself.

This is the child laid in a manger:
awareness arriving without credentials,
owing nothing to the past,
asking nothing of the future.

It is your birth.

Not the birth of your name or your story,
but the birth of bare experience itself —
appearing freely, freshly,
as if for the first time. And it is here, now, in every moment. 

Nothing needed to come before.
Nothing needs to come after.

Only this:

“*Here I am*” / Hineni (הִנְנִי)

And for some, like Samuel, this blossoms into:

“*Speak, for your servant is listening.*” 

Merry Christmas.
