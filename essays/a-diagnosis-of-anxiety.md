A diagnosis of anxiety

Anxiety can be understood not as a response to the future, but as the felt consequence of a specific cognitive operation: the attempt to complete a loop of identity within mind that cannot, in principle, be completed.

At its core, the mind functions by drawing distinctions and relating them hierarchically through reentry. What is called “memory” is not access to a past that still exists, but the construction of a present image upon the senses labeled as past. This operation succeeds because it is entirely contained within the present moment. The system refers to itself, and the loop closes.

From this, a crucial but subtle error arises. Because the reconstruction of the “past” appears to provide knowledge and continuity, the mind assumes that a similar operation can be applied forward: that the present can be projected into a “future,” and that this projection can be stabilized, secured, or resolved in advance. But this symmetry is false.

The past, as it appears in experience, is a present construction available for reentry. The future is not. There is no corresponding object to complete the loop. There is only a present thought about a future, which the system treats as if it were something to be acted upon or resolved.

Thus the mind attempts to project a future state, evaluate or control that projection, and achieve closure with respect to it. But because the future is not available for reentry, the loop cannot close.

This failure to close is not abstract. It is felt. The organism remains in a state of unresolved activation: anticipation without completion, evaluation without resolution, readiness without discharge. This ongoing, self-sustaining activation is what is experienced as anxiety.

Crucially, anxiety is not caused by the content of thought. Whether the projection concerns health, survival, relationships, or meaning, the structure remains identical. The content merely provides a surface onto which the process attaches. The underlying mechanism is invariant: an open identity loop, misperceived as one that must be closed.

This also explains the self-amplifying nature of anxiety. The system interprets the unresolved state as a sign that further effort is required. It increases analysis, planning, rehearsal, and avoidance. But each of these is itself a new projection into the future, and therefore re-instantiates the same unclosable loop. More effort does not resolve anxiety; it perpetuates the condition that generates it.

The central misinterpretation is this: the feeling of anxiety is taken as evidence that something is wrong in the future. In fact, the feeling is the direct consequence of attempting to operate on something that is not present and cannot be made present.

From this perspective, anxiety is neither a flaw nor a pathology in the usual sense. It is the natural outcome of a misapplied cognitive function: the extension of a valid reentrant operation (memory) into a domain where reentry is impossible (the future).

The resolution does not lie in solving the projected future, nor in perfecting the attempt at prediction. It lies in recognizing the structure of the operation itself. When it becomes clear that no future state is available to be secured in advance, and no projection can be completed ahead of its occurrence, the compulsion to close the loop begins to relax.

What remains is not the elimination of uncertainty, but the absence of the effort to eliminate it.

In its simplest form: anxiety is the felt signature of the mind attempting to inhabit a time that does not exist. And with the cessation of that attempt, what appeared as a problem is revealed to have been an activity… one that can, at any moment, come to rest.
