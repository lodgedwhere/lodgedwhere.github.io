**A confusion about where meaning lives**

The doctrine of “original sin” is traditionally interpreted as a moral failure, a disobedient act, or the emergence of pride. But there is a more structurally precise reading: original sin is not the introduction of distinction itself, but a specific misapplication of distinction.

The primordial distinction between “I” and “not-I” is not itself sinful. It is the minimal condition for experience, language, and worldhood to arise at all. Without it, there would be neither perception nor action. The fall does not occur with this distinction, but with a subsequent inference: the belief that further distinctions within not-I can reveal something essential about I.

In the Genesis narrative, the “tree of the knowledge of good and evil” is not a source of moral information. Rather, it represents \*the assumption that reality can be articulated in such a way that meaning or self-certainty can be extracted from it\*. “Eating the fruit” signifies the acceptance of a promise that sufficient discrimination, evaluation, and explanation of the world will culminate in self-understanding or completion.

Yet knowledge does not converge. Distinction proliferates. Each refinement generates further distinctions without terminus. The tree endlessly branches but never bears fruit. The sin, therefore, is not the acquisition of knowledge but the belief that knowledge is teleological, ie that it yields something final, something consumable, something that resolves the question of selfhood.

This misorientation has immediate consequences. Once the self is taken to be explainable through the world, valuation becomes unavoidable. Good and evil arise not as ethical categories but as existential measurements: favorable or unfavorable to “me”. Time becomes instrumental, oriented toward future resolution. Shame appears as the world is converted into a mirror for identity. Exile follows naturally, not as punishment, but as the experiential condition of a self that now seeks itself where it cannot be found.

Redemption, on this view, is not a return to pre-distinction innocence, nor the rejection of knowledge. It is the collapse of the false inference that the not-I can certify the I. When this belief falls away, the world is released from its burden of explanation. Knowledge may continue to grow, but it no longer functions as salvation.

Original sin, then, is not a crime committed in time. It is an epistemic misunderstanding: a confusion about where meaning lives.
