**Ego death is no death at all**

To hope, to fear, or even to believe in the logic of if/then is already to assume a self that lives in time. Hope says: if this happens, then I will be fulfilled. Fear says: if this happens, then I will suffer. In both cases the structure is identical. A future outcome is imagined, and a persistent “I” is assumed to exist there to receive it. At the same time, the past is interpreted as a chain of necessary preconditions, ie memories arranged into a story explaining why the present moment has the configuration it does. In this way the psychological self is woven entirely from past and future, in which the past provides narrative identity, and the future provides imagined resolution.

But the recognition of this structure occurs only Now.

When this is seen clearly, the ego faces an impossible situation. Its entire existence depends on maintaining the projection of conditional outcomes into the future, built from memory of the past, leapfrogging the present moment which is the only “knowable” source of truth. Yet, once this mechanism is fully recognized, the projection loses its apparent authority. If the ego continues projecting futures, suffering — especially the inevitability of annihilation in death — continues; if it stops, the structure that defines it collapses. In religious language the resolution appears as sacrifice: the self offers itself up for the sake of truth, or God, or the Kingdom.

Yet the sacrifice, once followed through in meditation or psychedelic states, reveals a curious fact. When the ego relinquishes itself and disappears, nothing in the reality of the present moment actually changes. The world within bare experience remains. Only the fictional claimant — the imagined center that believed it owned and controlled the unfolding of events — is gone, because it never existed in the present moment to begin with. It was always made up of phantoms generated by ignorant acceptance of an unsupported assumption underlying thought itself: if/then, the obviously false idea that the present moment can be something other than what already is.

Thus the annihilation the ego fears turns out to be strangely empty. Nothing has been destroyed except the assumption that there was someone to whom anything needed to happen. What remains is simply the immediacy of experience itself: this moment, unclaimed, unprojected, and never actually threatened in the first place.
