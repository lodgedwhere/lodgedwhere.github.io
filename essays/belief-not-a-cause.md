**Belief is not a cause**

Many contemporary nondual and quasi-nondual systems assert that belief or misperception gives rise to illusion, ego, or world-appearance. This paper argues that such claims commit a category error. Belief cannot function as a cause, because causality operates only within appearance, while belief conditions the very intelligibility of appearance. Treating belief as causal reintroduces the very dualism such systems aim to dissolve. The argument is developed formally and applied to the metaphysical core of A Course in Miracles (ACIM) as a representative case.

⸻

1. Causality and Its Domain

Causality is a relation defined within appearance. For a causal claim to be coherent, both relata (“cause” and “effect”) must be distinguishable appearances situated within a temporal framework. Formally, if A \\rightarrow B is a causal relation, then both A and B must be elements of the same domain of appearance.

This requirement is not controversial. One cannot coherently say that the rules of chess cause the chessboard or the pieces. Causes presuppose the domain in which they operate.

⸻

2. What Belief Is (and Is Not)

Belief is often treated psychologically, as a mental state or event. However, when belief is invoked to explain the existence or structure of the world itself — as in metaphysical or nondual cosmologies — it is no longer functioning as a psychological object.

In this context, belief is better understood as a positional commitment: a stance that fixes what counts as real, meaningful, threatening, illusory, or true. Belief is not one appearance among others; it is the condition under which appearances are interpreted as appearances at all.

Thus, belief is not an object in the world. Nor is it an event in time. It is logically prior to both.

⸻

3. The Category Error

From the above, the error becomes visible.

Proposition 1: Only appearances can function as causes.

Proposition 2: Belief, when invoked metaphysically, is not an appearance.

Conclusion: Belief cannot function as a cause.

To say “belief causes illusion” is therefore incoherent. It attempts to place a condition of appearance inside the domain it conditions, granting it causal agency within a framework it already presupposes.

This is not a subtle mistake. It is equivalent to claiming that grammatical rules cause sentences, rather than define what counts as a sentence in the first place.

⸻

4. Application to A Course in Miracles

A Course in Miracles (ACIM) asserts, in various formulations, that belief in separation gives rise to ego, world, and suffering, and that corrected belief dissolves these effects. Although ACIM simultaneously insists that “nothing has happened,” it nevertheless relies on a causal grammar in which belief functions as an operative force.

This creates a tension:

    If belief truly causes the world, then belief must be an event with ontological force, and something has happened; If nothing has happened, belief cannot be causal, and the explanatory role assigned to it collapses.

ACIM attempts to resolve this tension by declaring the causal story “symbolic.” However, symbolic language still functions structurally. The problem is not that the story is metaphorical; it is that it assigns causal power where only logical conditioning is possible.

⸻

5. Belief as Boundary Condition, Not Cause

The coherent alternative is to treat belief not as a cause but as a boundary condition. Boundary conditions do not produce outcomes; they delimit the space in which outcomes are intelligible.

On this view, belief does not cause illusion. It is the stance from which appearance is articulated as illusion or as reality. Thus, changing belief does not undo effects; it replaces one interpretive frame with another.

This preserves the experiential insight that shifts in belief radically alter lived experience, without committing to the metaphysical error of treating belief as a world-producing force.

⸻

6. Implications for Nondual Thought

Once belief is removed from the role of cause, several consequences follow:

    1.	No origin story is required for illusion;
    
    2.	No agent must “misuse” creative power;
    
    3.	No fall needs to be explained or repaired.

Appearance arises with distinction itself, not through error or misbelief. The attempt to explain illusion by appealing to belief is revealed as a pedagogical strategy rather than a metaphysical account.

⸻

7. Conclusion

Belief cannot be a cause without collapsing the distinction between condition and content. Nondual cosmologies that rely on belief as a generative mechanism inadvertently reintroduce dualism at the level of explanation. A structurally consistent nondual account must relinquish causal narratives of error and instead recognize distinction itself as sufficient for appearance.

The question then shifts—from “what caused the world?” to “what does distinction do?”, and the need for cosmological storytelling quietly disappears.
