Reentry and "existence"

A useful way to model “existence,” as it appears to mind, is to begin with a distinction drawn in an undivided field. This first act is not yet the creation of a thing, but a gesture of indication — an attempt to bound what is taken as “This.” Because the field is originally unmarked, this boundary cannot truly isolate anything. It does not separate an inside from an outside in any final sense; it only provisionally indicates a region. As such, the first distinction is inherently unstable. It attempts to name what has no true opposite, and so it cannot rest as a completed act.

Yet this instability does not terminate the process. Through reentry, the marked side of the distinction is taken again as a field within which further distinctions can be drawn. In this way, the failure of the initial boundary becomes the condition for continuation. Each distinction produces a local domain, and within that domain, new distinctions arise without interruption. The process does not proceed by securing a final ground, but by continuously regenerating provisional ones.

Within such a reentrant field, naming becomes possible. A distinction drawn and redrawn in a consistent manner begins to stabilize as a “thing.” But this stability is not the result of a successfully enclosed boundary. Rather, it emerges from repeated reidentification — a persistence of indication across successive acts of distinction. A “thing” is therefore not a bounded entity, but a pattern maintained through recursive naming.

The appearance of mind follows from a further development within this structure. Once a field has been established through reentry, a secondary distinction can arise within it. This distinction divides the field into a privileged term and a remainder. The privileged term is what is called “I.” It is not an agent or a substance, but a reference point — one side of a distinction that functions as the origin of further relations.

With the emergence of this “I / not-I” structure, the activity of distinction becomes relational. New distinctions are no longer merely drawn; they are compared. Differences within the field are registered relative to the “I.” This ongoing process of comparison is what may be called measurement. It gives rise to the phenomena ordinarily attributed to mind: perception as the registration of difference, evaluation as the weighting of difference, memory as the retention of patterns of difference, and anticipation as the projection of possible differences.

In this sense, mind is not an entity but a dynamic network of relations. It consists in the continuous comparison between a reference term (“I”) and the distinctions arising within the remainder of the field. Nothing within this process requires belief, intention, or agency. The apparent coherence of mind is the result of structural persistence, not the action of an internal subject.

That persistence, however, is not guaranteed by any single distinction. It is sustained by reentry. Reentry allows distinctions to be reiterated, the “I” to be reidentified, and relational comparisons to accumulate. Without it, each distinction would dissolve as soon as it appeared, and no structure could be maintained. Reentry operates silently, never appearing as an object within the field it supports, yet enabling the entire system of naming and relation.

Thus, what is called “existence” in this model is not a stable given, but the ongoing activity of distinction and reentry. What is called “mind” is the relational structure that arises when a distinction within that activity is taken as a reference point and used for comparison. And beneath both lies the unmarked field — not as a hidden substance, but as that which requires no distinction, no naming, and no continuation.
