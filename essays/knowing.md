**Knowing**

The problem of ego is not that it is ignorant in the conventional sense, but that it is built from a foundational confusion. It wants to know that it knows. It wants certainty not merely as contact with truth, but as proof of ownership: “I understand, I possess, I stand above.” But the moment there is someone claiming to know, knowing has already fractured into three parts—knower, known, and the act between them. A subject appears only by stepping outside the immediacy of awareness to take possession of it, and in doing so, it has already exiled itself from what it wishes to secure.

What is overlooked is that knowing is never an object. It cannot be acquired. It cannot be confirmed from outside. Before any “I” appears to experience, analyze, or claim insight, knowing is already fully present—self-shining, prior to subject and object, prior even to the totality of everything that can ever be known. To want to “have” knowing is to misunderstand it entirely. Knowing is not something one can hold; it is what one already is.

The ego cannot tolerate this. To simply be knowing, without standing apart from it, leaves nothing separate to admire, defend, justify, or perpetuate. So the ego invents a second-order stance: “I will think about thought. I will stand above experience and evaluate it. I will become the one who understands.” But this “higher vantage point” is not higher at all. It is only another thought folded back upon thought. Every symbol remains symbol; every abstraction remains within abstraction.

We tend to believe that thought examining thought rises toward purity, clarity, or reality. But recursion is not transcendence. It is only inward elaboration, the hall of mirrors turning upon itself. No matter how refined, symbolic reflection never escapes the order of symbol. It cannot leap out of mind by thinking about mind. It cannot purify itself by multiplying its reflections. What the mind constructs is never closer to reality simply because it is more complex, coherent, mystical, or philosophical. Thought about thought is only more thought.

This is the first assumption upon which the mind builds itself:
There exists a standpoint outside experience from which experience can be judged.

But that standpoint is itself imagined. It has never actually existed.

From this illusion arise metaphysics, theologies, grand systems, personal narratives, and spiritual striving. Civilization’s most impressive philosophical cathedrals rest on the conviction that by thinking harder, deeper, more recursively, we will eventually arrive at truth. Yet nothing inside a mirror ever becomes what the mirror reflects. Thought cannot reason its way into reality because it is already downstream of reality. It is secondary. Derivative. A gesture happening inside what is already whole.

There is nothing inherently wrong with symbol. The beauty lies not in abolishing thought, but in seeing its nature clearly. Thought is a luminous surface, a play of reflections, a living movement of form. The problem begins only when symbol claims sovereignty, when it imagines itself to be more true than immediacy, when representation pretends to outrank what it represents.

Humility begins when symbol recognizes its limits. When thought finally sees that it cannot touch the source of knowing, it stops reaching outward for certainty and stops folding endlessly inward to validate itself. It falls silent not in defeat, but in relief. The urge to “know that I know” dissolves because the one who would possess knowing is revealed to be nothing more than another movement inside knowing.

What remains is not a superior philosophy, not a new vantage point, not mystical insight owned by a spiritual self. What remains is simply **This**: immediacy prior to every symbol, prior to every interpretation, prior even to the sense of a self encountering it. Knowing knowing itself, with no one left to claim it.

And in that quiet clarity, the ego’s deepest longing is answered in the only way it ever could be: not by securing itself, but by disappearing into the simplicity of what has always been here.
