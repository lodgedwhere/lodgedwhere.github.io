**The inevitable self-defeat of technological progress**

Technological progress is a movement away from immediacy. Each new layer of invention (tools, language, writing, computation) places an additional veil between consciousness and the raw fact of experience. The world becomes mediated through symbols, models, and representations. What is directly present is gradually replaced by what is described, measured, predicted, and manipulated. In this sense, technology appears as the very engine of separation.

But this process does not begin with machines. It begins with language itself.

The first word is already a departure. To name something is to substitute a stable symbol for a fluid and irreducible appearance. The word allows recognition, memory, and communication, but it also introduces a subtle displacement. What is present is no longer simply encountered; it is interpreted. From this initial distinction, further structures unfold. Writing stabilizes language across time. Mathematics formalizes relationships. Science builds predictive systems. Digital technologies interconnect and accelerate these systems into vast, recursive networks of representation.

At each stage, mediation deepens. The world is no longer lived directly but accessed through layers of abstraction. The self, too, becomes entangled in this process. It emerges as a center within the symbolic system: a reference point that organizes meaning and action. Thus, the same movement that constructs a world of objects also constructs the subject that navigates them. Separation appears complete: a knower here, a known world there, linked only through representation.

This is the problem. Or rather, this is the appearance of the problem.

For what is actually displaced in this process? Not reality itself, but attention’s relationship to it. The immediacy of experience is never removed; it is only overlaid. The symbolic system does not replace the real, it obscures by presenting its own structures as if they were primary. The more effective the system, the more invisible this substitution becomes.

Yet here lies the paradox.

The same process that creates the illusion of separation also carries within it the conditions for its dissolution. At low levels of mediation, the system is convincing. It functions smoothly, and its constructed nature is easily overlooked. But as mediation intensifies layer upon layer, reentry upon reentry, it becomes increasingly difficult to ignore the fact that one is operating within a network of symbols referring largely to other symbols.

The system begins to reveal itself.

The self is seen as narrative rather than substance. Meaning is seen as relational rather than inherent. Knowledge is seen as a transformation of symbols rather than a direct grasp of reality. The more complete and powerful the system becomes, the more it exposes its own groundlessness. It cannot step outside itself to justify its claims, nor can it fully capture what it purports to describe.

In this way, technological progress functions as both the cause and the resolution of the illusion of separation. It constructs the distance by elaborating mediation, but it also makes that mediation so explicit, so pervasive, that its limits become undeniable. The illusion is not dismantled by retreating from technology or abandoning symbols. It becomes transparent when the symbolic system is driven to the point where it can no longer conceal its own nature.

Separation, then, is not undone by stripping away layers, but by saturating them.

When mediation is seen clearly as mediation, the presumed gap between consciousness and reality loses its footing. There is no need to bridge a distance that was never truly there. What remains is not a new understanding, but the simple recognition that immediacy was never displaced, only overlooked.

Thus, the arc of technological progress, beginning with the first word, traces a strange and circular path. It leads consciousness away from itself through ever more elaborate representations, only to return it, through the exhaustion of those representations, to what was present from the beginning.
