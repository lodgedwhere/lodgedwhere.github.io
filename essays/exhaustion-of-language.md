Exhaustion of language
*April 2026*

The closer one comes to saying what is true, the farther one moves from anyone who can hear it. This is not a failure of skill, nor a limitation of vocabulary. It is a structural feature of language itself.

Language begins with an unspoken axiom: that something can be conveyed from one to another. Every word, every distinction, every refinement presumes a shared field in which meaning can travel. Speaker and listener stand apart, and language bridges the gap. This assumption is so fundamental that it is rarely noticed. It is simply what language is for.

At first, precision appears to serve this purpose. We refine our words to eliminate ambiguity, to narrow meaning, to approach truth more closely. But as this process continues, something unexpected occurs. Each refinement introduces finer distinctions, and each distinction depends on assumptions that are neither stated nor shared. What seemed like clarification begins to expose divergence. The listener no longer hears what is meant, but only what their own framework can accommodate.

As language approaches its limit—when it attempts to describe what is prior to all distinction—it encounters a deeper contradiction. That which is undivided cannot be spoken of without dividing it. To describe it is to move away from it. Precision, in this domain, does not converge on truth. It multiplies the very structures that obscure it.

And so language, taken to its extreme, negates itself. It becomes perfectly exact and perfectly incommunicable at once. It fulfills its own rules so completely that it dissolves the condition that made communication possible. There is nothing wrong with the statement; there is simply no longer a shared ground in which it can be understood.

What we call misunderstanding is not an error in this process. It is its natural outcome. Understanding relies on overlap, on approximation, on the soft edges of imprecision. When those edges are sharpened, the illusion of shared meaning falls away. What remains cannot be transferred, because it was never an object to begin with.

In this way, language performs its final function not by delivering truth, but by exhausting the attempt to express it. It reveals, through its own collapse, that what is most real does not pass between speaker and listener. It does not travel. It is not conveyed.

The closer one comes to saying it, the farther one moves from anyone who can hear it. And in that distance, something is uncovered that no statement can contain, and no listener can receive.
