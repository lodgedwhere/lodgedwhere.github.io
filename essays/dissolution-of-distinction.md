#Dissolution of distinction#

The existence of any distinction implies a first distinction. For meaning to arise at all, something must be set apart from something else. Yet the creation of meaning that drives further elaboration of distinction requires more than mere difference; it requires orientation. The primordial cut is not simply between this and that, but between I and not-I. Only with this asymmetry does distinction become generative, giving rise to recognition, reference, and the unfolding of thought.

The minimal “language” that describes this primordial structure is isomorphic to K₄, the fully connected graph of four elements. In this context, those elements can be understood as Fold (I / not-I), Unfold (not-I), Paradox (not-I / I), and Ouroboros (I). This fourfold captures the complete set of possibilities generated by a single distinction under reentry into the marked (“I”) side, when the distinction is applied to itself.

When this graph is taken to apply not locally but to the Whole of experience, it collapses. Two of its nodes (Unfold and Ouroboros) eliminate the distinction entirely. If everything is not-I, or everything is I, the distinction has no operative meaning — a name applied to the Whole does not stand outside it — and the graph annihilates itself, along with its witness. The remaining two nodes (Fold and Paradox) are degenerate. There is no structural difference between reentering one side of the distinction or the other; the two are indistinguishable. As a result, the identification of one side as “I” rather than “not-I” is fundamentally ungrounded. The distinction remains syntactically available (it can be drawn, named, and manipulated) but it becomes semantically inert. It no longer secures a determinate reference, and stands as an irreducible, indeterminate oscillation.

Despite this operational ambiguity, the distinction persists. Every subsequent act of thought, perception, or recognition presupposes and re-enacts the original cut. This first distinction is logically prior, though not temporally so; it is not an event in the past but a structural condition continually reinstantiated. Each new distinction silently reaffirms the asymmetry between I and not-I, even though that asymmetry has no foundation. In this way, all acts of mind perpetuate an ungrounded assumption.

The apparent stability of this structure is sustained through mutual reinforcement. The identities, roles, and meanings that populate human life — “myself,” “my life,” “my rights,” “my property” — are not independently grounded truths, but interdependent constructions. They persist as stories, each supported by others, forming a network of shared distinctions that constitute society, culture, and civilization. Though tensions arise between one story and another, none stands alone; each relies on the coherence of the whole. Like reflections between mirrors without an original, or the jewels of Indra’s Net, they arise mutually and sustain one another without any underlying referent.

Thus the entire edifice of meaning rests on a distinction that cannot justify its own orientation. The language that arises from it continues to function, but without ultimate support. What appears as a stable world of subjects and objects, selves and others, is the ongoing re-enactment of a primordial cut whose asymmetry is both necessary and ultimately groundless.
