**Gauge freedom and "me"**

Any homogeneous, isotropic space contains no fundamentally privileged origin. No point is the center; all points are equivalent. Yet to say anything at all about what happens in such a space (ie to describe motion, distance, or change) we must nevertheless choose a reference point. This choice does not reveal a fact about the space itself. It overlays a coordinate system on an originless reality in order to generate \*relative truths\*: statements about relations among contents, not about the space as such. The origin is introduced not because it is real, but because \*description itself is relational\*.

Physics provides a familiar illustration of this structure in Galilean invariance. In Newtonian mechanics, the laws of motion do not single out any preferred state of rest. Uniform motion has no absolute meaning; only motion relative to a chosen frame can be described. The arbitrariness of the reference frame is not a limitation of our knowledge but a constraint built into the theory itself. Absolute rest is not merely unknown, it is \*undefined\*. The necessity of choosing a frame is thus pragmatic, while the non-privileged nature of all frames is fundamental.

This same structure appears, with existential consequences, in lived experience. Experience/consciousness unfolds as a kind of formless “space” within which sensations, thoughts, and perceptions arise. For experience to be articulated at all, it must appear from somewhere, ie from a point within that space. But this point of view is not absolutely correct or ontologically privileged; it is simply the standpoint from which relations are registered. The perspective is fundamentally arbitrary, just as any spatial origin is arbitrary in a homogeneous field.

The difficulty arises when local phenomena (sensations in a body, thoughts in a mind, memories forming a narrative, etc) are used as reference markers to mistake that perspective for something intrinsic, to \*reify\* it. What began as a convenient coordinate choice is quietly upgraded into a supposed absolute center: “me”. Yet the phenomena used to anchor this center are impermanent. They change, decay, and disappear. Using them to anchor identity therefore produces an experience of instability. The underlying assumption (that there exists a fixed perspective which must persist) is incompatible with the very nature of the phenomena enlisted to support it! This mismatch is experienced as anxiety, grasping, and suffering. Impermanence is not the problem; expecting invariance where none exists is.

A more technically sophisticated but structurally precise analogue appears in black hole physics. In the standard coordinate system used to describe a non-rotating black hole, the event horizon appears as a singularity: time seems to freeze, and physical quantities diverge. However, this “singularity” is not a feature of spacetime itself. It is an artifact of the coordinate system, which implicitly privileges the viewpoint of a distant, stationary observer and attempts to extend that perspective into a region where its assumptions no longer apply. When the spacetime is described using a coordinate system that does not privilege any particular observer, the horizon is revealed to be entirely regular. Nothing catastrophic happens there at all. The infinity vanishes with the assumption that produced it.

The parallel with experience is disarmingly similar. Existential fear (ie, the sense of an impending void, collapse, or annihilation) arises when a framework that presupposes an absolute center is pushed beyond its domain of validity. The fear is not evidence of a metaphysical danger, but of a descriptive failure. It is a coordinate singularity, not a feature of reality. When the hidden assumption of a privileged perspective is relinquished, experience continues unimpeded. Appearances still arise and pass. What disappears is not the world, but the fictitious problem implied by an inapplicable frame demanding a persistent “me”.

Across physics and experience alike, the lesson is the same: reference frames are indispensable locally, but none are fundamental. Confusing the convenience of an origin with an absolute center generates paradox, instability, and false crises. Seeing the arbitrariness of the coordinate choice does not erase the phenomena; rather, it dissolves the suffering that came from mistaking description for reality.
